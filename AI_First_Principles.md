# AI First Principles
*Jeff Frazier | March 2026*

## Premise

Do not attempt an AI transformation — of a company, a portfolio, or a technology — without understanding the forces that govern it.

These are first principles *about AI* that anyone undertaking transformation must reckon with. They are not a transformation playbook. They are the governing truths that determine whether a transformation succeeds or fails, moves fast or stalls, creates durable value or temporary advantage.

Ignore any one of them and the work will surprise you.

## What Qualifies as a First Principle?

An irreducible truth about how AI changes economic value, competition, or organizational structure — one that materially governs the outcome of any effort to incorporate AI into an organization.

**The test for inclusion:**
- **Irreducible** — cannot be derived from another principle in this document
- **Universal** — not specific to an industry, company, or moment in time
- **Governing** — ignoring it predictably leads to failure or wasted effort
- **Falsifiable** — it makes a claim about reality that could be wrong

Everything else — taxonomies, frameworks, transition methodologies, applications — belongs in derived documents, not here.

---

## The Axiom

**The marginal cost of reproducible cognitive labor is approaching zero.**

Knowledge retrieval, pattern-based analysis, codifiable expertise, code, content — anything that can be specified and repeated — AI produces at near-zero marginal cost per unit.

This is the economic force that makes AI transformation necessary — and inevitable. Every principle that follows derives from it.

---

## Principle 2: Generation and Validation Are Different Cost Structures

The cost of *generating* intelligence approaches zero. The cost of *validating* it does not.

In any domain where errors carry consequences — patient harm, regulatory action, financial liability — the validation layer is substantial, skilled, and does not trend to zero. The higher the stakes, the wider the gap between generation cost and total deployment cost.

This is not a caveat to the Axiom. It is a separate economic fact — and the one most often underestimated by organizations beginning a transformation. It determines where AI deploys quickly, where it deploys slowly, and what the transformation actually costs.

---

## Principle 3: Value Migrates to the Irreproducible

When the cost of reproducing something approaches zero, the value of that thing approaches zero. Value concentrates in whatever remains scarce and hard to replicate.

**Reproducible at near-zero cost:** code, analysis, content, pattern-based expertise, standard designs, any capability describable as "it does X."

**Not reproducible:** proprietary data generated through operations, regulatory trust earned over years, physical operations and infrastructure, exclusive contractual relationships, network effects, validated compliance status.

Any transformation that strengthens reproducible capabilities is building on sand. The test for any asset: can a well-resourced competitor with AI reproduce this in months? If yes, it is not durable. Point the transformation at what is.

---

## Principle 4: The Frontier of Reproducibility Moves

"Irreproducible" is not a fixed category. It is a shrinking frontier.

What is hard to reproduce in 2026 may be trivially reproducible in 2028. The Axiom doesn't arrive all at once — it arrives in waves, each one collapsing the cost of a more complex category of cognitive labor. The sequence is roughly predictable: routine tasks fall first, then pattern-based expertise, then complex judgment, then novel reasoning under ambiguity. Physical operations and regulatory trust erode last, if at all.

**Critical observation:** The technology frontier is ahead of the institutional frontier. The capability to perform cross-domain synthesis, multi-source strategic reasoning, and scenario-based judgment under uncertainty already exists — a single practitioner with domain expertise and AI fluency can build what would have required large specialized teams 18 months ago. What lags is organizational willingness to trust, adopt, and restructure around these capabilities. The waves below reflect when these capabilities become *widely reproducible by ordinary organizations* — not when a skilled practitioner can first achieve them.

**The collapse sequence:**

| Wave | What becomes reproducible | What falls in value | Approximate horizon |
|------|--------------------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| **Already here** | Content, code, basic analysis, research, translation, first-draft everything, pattern-based domain expertise, structured decision-making, multi-step workflows | Junior and mid-level knowledge workers, commodity software, standard reports, routine consulting, most analyst roles | 2024–2026 |
| **Here for practitioners, arriving for organizations** | Cross-domain synthesis, multi-source strategic reasoning, scenario modeling, complex recommendation with context, graph-based analysis, Monte Carlo-scored decision support | Senior consultants, specialized advisory, most professional services not protected by regulation or physical presence | 2026–2028 |
| **Approaching** | Autonomous judgment in bounded domains, scientific hypothesis generation, real-time adaptive strategy | Roles requiring novelty detection within documented domains, high-volume expert judgment | 2027–2030 |
| **Uncertain** | Autonomous judgment under genuine ambiguity, physical-world reasoning, open-ended scientific discovery | Roles requiring truly unprecedented reasoning with no documented precedent | 2030+ |
| **Last to fall (if ever)** | Physical operations, earned regulatory trust, human relationships, political capital, embodied expertise | Manufacturing, clinical care, regulatory relationships, network effects rooted in trust | Unknown |

The gap between "a practitioner can do this" and "an organization routinely does this" is the adoption lag — and it is where the transformation opportunity lives. The company that closes this gap fastest in its industry captures the value before the market reprices.

**The strategic implication:** When evaluating any asset, capability, or competitive position, the question is not "is this reproducible?" but "is this reproducible *on the relevant time horizon?*" For a PE firm with a 3–5 year hold period, the answer must account for which wave arrives during the hold. The timelines are shorter than most institutions expect.

## Principle 5: Advantage Compounds Only Through Proprietary Feedback Loops

Adopting AI creates temporary advantage. Your competitor buys the same tools and the market reprices.

Durable advantage requires deploying AI into live operations so it generates proprietary data — predictions scored against outcomes, recommendations validated against results — that feeds back into the model continuously. A competitor who starts later can purchase the same tools but cannot purchase your accumulated operational data.

The advantage is not in having AI. It is in the irreproducible data generated by running AI against reality, over time. This means the transformation itself — the act of going live — is the moat-building activity. You cannot build the moat first and then transform. Transforming is what builds it.

---

## Principle 6: Changing the Cognitive Agent Changes the Architecture

Every workflow encodes the cognitive constraints of the agent performing it. Human workflows evolved around serial processing, limited working memory, fatigue, context-switching cost, and variable-weighting limits.

AI shares none of these constraints. AI has different constraints: hallucination, no genuine judgment under novel ambiguity, no physical capability.

Inserting AI into a human-optimized workflow preserves bottlenecks AI doesn't need and adds no guardrails for failures AI does have. Most failed transformations fail here — not because the AI was wrong, but because it was placed inside an architecture designed for a different kind of mind.

The correct question: if we built this process today with AI as the cognitive agent and humans handling judgment, novelty, and physical work — what would it look like?

---

## Principle 7: The Bottleneck Shifts from Execution to Direction

When execution — building, analyzing, coding, drafting — is near-free, execution stops being the bottleneck. The constraint becomes knowing what to build and why.

Domain expertise — deep understanding of a problem space, its stakeholders, its failure modes — is the new scarcity. Technical capability is the old one. The organization that puts AI directly in the hands of domain experts will outperform the one that routes domain problems through a technical team.

A transformation led by technologists who don't know the domain will build the wrong things. A transformation led by domain experts with AI in hand will find opportunities the technologists would never see.
