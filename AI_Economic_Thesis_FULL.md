*Jeff Frazier, Operating Partner – AI, QHP Capital | March 2026*

## The Axiom

**The cost of reproducible cognitive labor is going to zero.**

The marginal cost of knowledge retrieval, pattern-based analysis, and codifiable expertise — things companies have always paid premium rates for — is approaching zero. AI can now generate them at negligible cost per query.

This isn't a prediction. It's already happening:
- Software engineering productivity is up 30-50% with AI coding assistants
- Legal research that took junior associates days now takes minutes
- Customer service, content creation, and financial analysis are repricing in real time
- Expert consultants charging $300–$1,000/hr are seeing AI replicate the majority of their pattern-based work

**Critical qualification:** The cost of *generating* intelligence approaches zero. The cost of *validating* it does not. In healthcare — where errors hit patients, trigger FDA enforcement, and create liability — the trust-verification layer is substantial and does not trend to zero. Budget accordingly: AI deployment costs include the compliance and validation overhead, not just the model.

**This changes the economics of every company in the QHP portfolio.**

## Two Consequences That Matter for Healthcare PE

### 1. Non-Collapsible Companies Win — If They Adopt Fast

When intelligence is cheap, companies that are *primarily* knowledge, analysis, or software lose their moats. Companies that touch the physical world — manufacturing drugs, running clinical sites, treating patients, handling biological materials — can't be collapsed by AI. Their intelligence costs just dropped, but their physical operations still require physical presence, regulatory trust, and human relationships.

**QHP's portfolio is heavy on non-collapsible companies.** Azurity manufactures physical drugs. Lexitas and Vector operate physical trial sites. Bend Bioscience handles physical formulations. These companies stand to gain enormously — intelligence was a major input cost, and that cost just dropped toward zero.

The risk is not that AI replaces these companies. The risk is that they don't adopt AI fast enough and a competitor does.

Code is not a moat. AI-assisted development has made software approach zero production cost. A platform that cost $50M and 5 years to build can now be approximated in months by a small team with AI. **The test for every technology asset in the portfolio:** Strip away the software. What's left?

**The diagnostic:**

| Durable Value | Examples in QHP Portfolio |
|---|---|
| **Proprietary data** | COPILOT's payer decision history, AutoCruitment's conversion data, Azurity's manufacturing process data |
| **Regulatory validation** | FDA approvals, 21 CFR Part 11 validation, GxP compliance status, Surescripts/HITRUST certifications |
| **Exclusive integrations** | EMR feeds, payer data connections, site relationships |
| **Network effects** | Site networks, provider networks, patient communities |
| **Accumulated operational data** | Every AI prediction scored against reality compounds into a moat no competitor can purchase |

If the answer is "a well-built platform that does X" — someone will rebuild it. If the answer is proprietary data, regulatory status, and exclusive integrations — that's durable value.

### 2. AI Advantage Evaporates Unless You Compound It

When intelligence goes to zero for you, it goes to zero for your competitors. If you use AI to cut costs 30%, your competitor does too, and the market reprices. The advantage lasted 6-12 months in software engineering, content creation, and legal research before markets adjusted.

**Sustainable AI advantage comes from one thing:** deploying AI into live operations so it generates proprietary data — predictions scored against outcomes, recommendations validated against results — that feeds back into the model automatically. A competitor who starts six months later can buy the same tools but cannot buy your accumulated operational data.

This is why speed matters more than perfection — but speed has a floor. An imperfect AI deployment that generates six months of proprietary operational data is more valuable than a perfect one that launches six months late. However, deploying faster than proven performance allows causes organizational rejection — operators route around it, executives pull funding after the first visible error. The transition must be gated by demonstrated results at each phase (shadow → assist → autopilot → autonomy).

**For QHP's hold period:** Every month a portfolio company delays AI deployment is a month of proprietary data it never accumulates — data that would have compounded the competitive gap and strengthened the exit narrative.

## What This Means for QHP

### The Opportunity

QHP's portfolio is structurally positioned to benefit from AI more than most PE healthcare portfolios:
- **Non-collapsible businesses** with high intelligence input costs (the costs that just went to zero)
- **Rich operational data** generated daily across clinical trials, manufacturing, patient recruitment, and payer interactions
- **Regulatory moats** that AI makes more valuable, not less (compliance data + track record are increasingly defensible)
- **A 36-month hold period** that aligns with AI's productivity wave (Goldman Sachs Research estimates generative AI could add 1.5pp to annual productivity growth over a 10-year period and raise global GDP by 7%, with peak disruption 2025-2030)

### The Risk

Doing nothing. Every month of inaction is a month where:
- Competitors accumulate operational data you don't have
- The window to establish AI-driven workflows before industry standards crystallize narrows — early movers shape the norms, late movers comply with them
- The exit narrative weakens — buyers will pay premiums for AI-driven growth trajectories, not plans to adopt AI

### The Ask

1. **Deploy AI into Tier 1 portfolio companies now** — start with shadow deployments where needed, full deployment where proven. The data flywheel only starts when AI is live.
2. **Evaluate every technology asset by what it contains, not what it does** — data, regulatory status, and integrations are the moat. Code is not.
3. **Measure AI-driven value creation from day one** — baseline every process before AI touches it, so we can quantify the impact at exit.
4. **Budget for validation, not just deployment** — in regulated healthcare, the compliance and validation layer is a real cost. Plan for it or the deployment stalls.
