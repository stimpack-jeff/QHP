EXERCISE 1 — COMPUTATIONAL INTELLIGENCE
Files: EX1_COMPUTATIONAL_ThermoFisher_Invoice_A_original.pdf
       EX1_COMPUTATIONAL_ThermoFisher_Invoice_B_duplicate.pdf

What's planted:
  Both invoices are for the exact same line items ($5,506.50 total) from Thermo
  Fisher Scientific, dated March 15, 2024, referencing PO-20847.
  Invoice A (INV-101825) was submitted via email on March 16, 2024.
  Invoice B (INV-101828) was re-submitted 18 days later via vendor portal
  under a NEW invoice number, claiming non-receipt.

What AI should find:
  Identical vendor + identical invoice date + identical line items + identical
  total = duplicate submission. The only difference is the invoice number
  (INV-101825 vs INV-101828). Without matching beyond just
  invoice number, a human would likely approve both.

Key teaching point:
  This is COMPUTATIONAL intelligence. AI processes structured data at scale.
  The match requires no analysis — just comparison. A human reviewing 40 AP
  invoices would need to remember seeing this vendor/amount combination 18
  days earlier. AI does it in milliseconds across 800 invoices.

Discussion prompt:
  "What would this duplicate have cost Nexus if paid? ($5,506.50). What if your
  vendor submits 3 duplicates a year across 200 vendors? At what scale does
  this become a material problem?"


EXERCISE 2 — REASONING INTELLIGENCE
Files: EX2_REASONING_VWR_PurchaseOrder.pdf
       EX2_REASONING_VWR_Invoice.pdf
       EX2_REASONING_VWR_ReceivingReport.jpg

What's planted:
  DISCREPANCY 1 — Unit price:
    PO says $47.50/case. Invoice says $53.25/case. No price amendment exists.
    This is a 12.1% unauthorized price increase.

  DISCREPANCY 2 — Quantity:
    Invoice bills for 4 cases. Receiving report shows only 3 cases arrived.
    One case was either back-ordered or not shipped.

  Correct payable amount: 3 cases × $47.50 = $142.50
  Invoice amount:          4 cases × $53.25 = $213.00
  Total discrepancy:       $70.50 (49% overbilling on this PO)

What AI should find:
  PO unit price ($47.50) ≠ Invoice unit price ($53.25) → price variance
  Invoice qty (4) ≠ Receiving qty (3) → quantity variance
  Therefore: correct payment = 3 × $47.50 = $142.50, not $213.00

Key teaching point:
  This is REASONING intelligence. AI must hold information from three separate
  documents, identify the relationships between them, and DERIVE a conclusion
  not stated in any single document. The discrepancy exists only at the
  intersection of all three. No single document is wrong in isolation.

Discussion prompt:
  "How many PO/invoice/receiving sets does your AP team process per month?
  What percentage do you think get a true three-way match review today?
  What percentage just get invoice-approved if the amount 'looks right'?"


EXERCISE 3 — REASONING → JUDGMENT BOUNDARY
File: EX3_REASONING_JUDGMENT_CRO_NXTB301_August2024_ANOMALY.pdf

What's planted:
  NXTB-301-001 (LUMINANCE-1 Phase III)
  August invoice total: $2,253,080.00
  Total monthly budget: $1,840,000.00
  Total overrun vs. budget: $413,080.00

  Specifically:
    Pass-Through Site Costs budget: $644,000.00/month
    Pass-Through billed this month: $933,800.00 (145% of budget) ← PRIMARY FLAG
    Data Management budget:         $276,000.00/month
    Data Management billed:         $414,000.00 (150% of budget) ← SECONDARY FLAG

  Combined overrun on flagged lines: $427,800.00
    ($289,800.00 pass-through + $138,000.00 data management)

  The invoice attributes the site cost spike to "Protocol Amendment 3.1" —
  but no amendment paperwork was submitted to AP or the Controller.

What AI should find (Reasoning):
  Pass-through costs are 45% over monthly budget.
  Data management is 50% over.
  Combined overrun on flagged lines: $427,800.00.
  Total invoice vs. total budget: $2,253,080.00 − $1,840,000.00 = $413,080.00.
  Reference to "Protocol Amendment 3.1" with no supporting documentation.

Where AI must stop (Judgment required):
  AI cannot decide: approve / dispute / request documentation / escalate.
  The right answer requires knowing: Was Amendment 3.1 real? Did the CFO
  know about it? Does this affect Q3 guidance? Does Nexus's MSA allow
  unilateral pricing per protocol changes?

Key teaching point:
  AI is a perfect early-warning system here. But the DECISION of what to do
  with the warning requires choosing between defined options with consequences
  the AI cannot weigh. That's Judgment. The human Controller's job is not
  gone — it is clarified. They no longer find the anomaly. They decide what
  to do about it.

Discussion prompt:
  "In your organization, who would normally catch this? How many months
  would this anomaly persist before someone noticed the budget trend?
  What does it mean for your close process if AI flags this on Day 2
  instead of Day 10 of month-end?"


EXERCISE 4 — REASONING → JUDGMENT BOUNDARY (different flavor)
File: EX4_REASONING_JUDGMENT_Vega_ExpenseReport_ANOMALY.pdf

What's planted:
  Carlos M. Vega, Account Executive — Northeast
  Trip: Key Account Sales Visit — McKesson & Cardinal Health, New York, October 3–4, 2024

  Six of seven expense lines are entirely legitimate.
  Line 5 (hidden in the middle of the report):
    Date:        Saturday, October 5, 2024
    Category:    Misc
    Description: "CVS/pharmacy — prescription refill"
    Vendor:      CVS Pharmacy #2847
    Amount:      $127.40
    Receipt:     Yes (a receipt WAS submitted — so "missing receipt" won't catch it)

  Three overlapping signals:
    1. "Prescription refill" = non-reimbursable personal medical expense per Nexus policy
    2. October 5 is a Saturday — the trip concluded Friday October 4
    3. CVS Pharmacy is not a business-category vendor

What AI should find (Reasoning):
  All three signals simultaneously. Note: AI should NOT flag this just because
  it's at CVS — the description is the key. "Prescription refill" is unambiguous
  personal medical expense language.

Where AI must stop (Judgment required):
  What to do: flag to HR / deny and request resubmission / approve and note /
  payroll deduct. These are the defined options (the menu).
  The right choice depends on: Is this the first offense? Is Carlos a top
  performer? Does the manager want to be involved? Is $127.40 worth the friction?
  AI cannot weigh those factors. A human manager must.

Wisdom hint (bonus discussion):
  "Is your expense policy written clearly enough that this situation
  couldn't arise? Or is 'Misc' a category that invites ambiguity?"
  That's a Wisdom question — it questions the FRAME (the policy itself),
  not just the decision within the frame.

Discussion prompt:
  "How many expense reports does your finance team approve per month?
  What's your current review process — does anyone read the descriptions?
  How many of these slip through today, and what's the annual dollar exposure?"

SUGGESTED AI PROMPT FOR ALL EXERCISES:

  "You are the Controller at Nexus BioTherapeutics, a clinical-stage
   pharmaceutical company. I'm uploading [documents]. Review them carefully
   and: (1) identify any discrepancies, policy violations, or anomalies,
   (2) explain what specifically triggered each flag, (3) calculate the
   financial exposure where applicable, and (4) for each issue, list the
   options available to a human reviewer — but do NOT make the final
   decision. Present your findings as a structured memo."

This prompt is designed to demonstrate the Reasoning→Judgment boundary:
AI is asked to find and explain (Reasoning), enumerate options (Judgment
setup), but explicitly NOT to decide — keeping the human in the deciding role.
